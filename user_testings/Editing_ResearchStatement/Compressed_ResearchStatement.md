**Popular Government after the Guzmanian State: Participatory Constitution-Making, Fiscal Constraint, and Digital Mediation in Chile**

**1. Introduction & Research Objectives**
Chile’s post-2019 constitutional process represents a critical conjuncture for studying the transition from democratic revolt to institutional refoundation. The process promised to replace the institutional architecture inherited from the Pinochet dictatorship—a state form shaped by Jaime Guzmán’s project of protected democracy, constrained sovereignty, and anti-majoritarian insulation (the "Guzmanian state"). However, the expansive constitutional draft produced by the 2021–2022 Convention failed to secure majority legitimacy, resulting in a decisive plebiscitary rejection. 

This project investigates the gap between participatory opening and plebiscitary rejection. The central research question asks: *How did Chile’s 2021–2022 constitution-making process transform a long crisis of institutional legitimacy into an expansive constitutional proposal, and why did that participatory opening fail to consolidate a politically plausible project of popular government?* The objective is to explain the failed translation from participatory mobilization into durable constitutional authority, focusing on the friction between citizen input, representative mediation, and elite contestation.

**2. Hypothesis & Theoretical Framework (Track 1 Alignment)**
The central hypothesis is that this failure resulted from intense power struggles and competing visions of democratic legitimacy, catalyzed by the interaction of historical disconnection, macro-fiscal constraints, and digitally mediated public discourse. The Convention expanded participatory mechanisms, bringing historically excluded actors into the institutional field, but these innovations clashed with established elite networks over representation, accountability, and the boundaries of democratic authority. 

Rather than explaining the outcome through the single lenses of institutional design or misinformation, this project examines how these elements interact. By exploring how fiscal pressures and digital campaigns reshaped constitutional legitimacy claims, the project seeks to **bridge the gap between normative theories and empirical evidence**. It theorizes that participatory constitution-making is not a neutral exercise in deliberation but a site of profound power struggles, where the ultimate consequences for representation, accountability, and governability are determined by the interaction of institutional rules, material constraints, and communicative channels.

**3. Empirical Strategy & Data Access**
The empirical strategy relies on the systematic collection of standardized variables across three archives: 
1. *The Bachelet II baseline (2015–2018)*: Local and territorial dialogues, cabildos, and the 2018 constitutional draft.
2. *The 2021–2022 Convention corpus*: Committee materials, popular initiatives, debate transcripts, and the final proposed charter.
3. *The digital public sphere*: Campaigns, Facebook Ad Library records, and public opinion polls.

To ensure feasibility and bypass commercial Twitter/X API access restrictions and prohibitive costs, the digital archive will utilize the Meta Ad Library, open-access datasets (such as the Spanish-language constitutional debates dataset hosted by Gelt.vc), and pre-scraped academic partner repositories. These archives will be coded into standardized variables designed to integrate directly with the POPGOV database, ensuring that the Chilean case is not analyzed in isolation but contributes to **building and analyzing a database** of constitutional design.

**4. Bounded Methodology**
The research combines comparative-historical process tracing of institutional design and legitimacy claims with computational text-as-data analysis. Process tracing will reconstruct the sequence linking the exhaustion of the Guzmanian state, the 2019 revolt, the constitutional opening, Convention deliberation under economic adjustment, and the referendum outcome. 

To ensure feasibility for a post-doctoral project, the computational text-as-data analysis will use standard NLP toolkits, employing topic modeling and semantic similarity measures to trace how specific constitutional demands migrated from citizen forums into the final draft, and how they were subsequently contested. Rather than executing complex dynamic time-series econometric modeling, the political-economic layer will be analyzed through a bounded, descriptive index of macro-fiscal indicators (growth, inflation, debt, and public expenditure) mapped chronologically against public opinion data and Convention debates. This approach allows us to examine how economic shocks and material constraints shape competing claims of democratic legitimacy and governability.

**5. Timeline & Database Integration (1+3 Years)**
This project is structured around the fellowship's 1+3 year format, ensuring a bounded, high-value deliverable at the end of the initial 12 months:
* **Year 1 (Bounded Phase)**: Focuses exclusively on coding the 2021–2022 Convention corpus and compiling the 2022 macro-fiscal conjuncture. The deliverable is the "Chilean Case Module" dataset, designed in collaboration with the POPGOV team so its variables (such as citizen submission rules, veto points, and media campaign indicators) map directly onto the cross-national POPGOV codebook.
* **Years 2–4 (Extension Phase)**: Expands the temporal scope to the Bachelet II baseline, incorporates the broader digital campaign archive, and integrates the Chilean module into cross-national comparative research to identify **patterns, drivers, and outcomes across diverse political contexts**.

**6. Interdisciplinary Value & Digital Infrastructure**
This research offers significant interdisciplinary value by bridging economics, data science, and sociology. As a political economist, my quantitative skill set allows me to translate normative debates about representation and economic rights into empirical database variables. 

Finally, the project contributes to the understanding of popular government in the digital age. Moving away from abstract cybernetic models, it links historical technological experiments—specifically the cybernetic feedback loops of Allende’s Project Cybersyn—to modern digital participatory platforms (like Decidim and the Chile Convención portals). It analyzes how the technical design of these platforms affects power struggles between elite actors and citizen movements, evaluating what institutional, fiscal, and digital capacities are required for popular government to translate participatory input into durable democratic authority.
