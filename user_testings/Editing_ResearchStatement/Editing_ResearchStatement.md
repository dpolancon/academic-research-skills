**Popular Government after the Guzmanian State: Participatory Constitution-Making, Fiscal Constraint, and Digital Mediation in Chile**

Chile’s first constitutional attempt after the 2019 revolt opened a rare democratic possibility. Ordinary citizens, social movements, elected representatives, experts, parties, and organized interests entered a process that promised to replace the institutional architecture inherited from dictatorship. The result was harder to interpret. A process born from mass mobilization and democratic refoundation produced an expansive constitutional proposal, but that proposal failed to secure majority legitimacy in the exit plebiscite.

This gap between participatory opening and plebiscitary rejection is the problem at the center of my proposed research. Chile shows that participation does not become popular government by institutional design alone. Citizen input must be translated into constitutional form, constitutional form into governing capacity, and governing capacity into a public sense that transformation is legitimate, coherent, and feasible. The first Chilean attempt after 2019 broke down somewhere along that chain.

The crisis did not begin with the Convention. It emerged from the long exhaustion of the post-1980 constitutional order: a state form shaped by Jaime Guzmán’s project of protected democracy, constrained sovereignty, subsidiary statehood, and anti-majoritarian insulation. I refer to this architecture as the Guzmanian state, not to reduce Chilean neoliberalism to one figure, but to name the institutional form through which neoliberal accumulation, democratic restriction, and reform without rupture were held together for four decades. By 2019, that order had not simply lost popularity. It had lost much of its capacity to process social conflict through ordinary reform.

The research asks how Chile’s 2021--2022 constitution-making process transformed a long crisis of institutional legitimacy into an expansive constitutional proposal, and why that participatory opening failed to consolidate a politically plausible project of popular government. The outcome to be explained is not participation itself. It is the failed translation from participatory mobilization into constitutional authority: the difficulty of turning citizen demands into a coherent institutional project capable of securing majority legitimacy, implementation credibility, and governing capacity.

My central hypothesis is that this failure resulted from intense power struggles and competing visions of democratic legitimacy, which were catalyzed by the interaction of historical disconnection, material constraints, and digitally mediated public discourse. The Convention expanded participatory mechanisms and opened a new space for constitutional imagination, yet these innovations clashed with existing elite networks and institutional structures over representation, accountability, and the boundaries of democratic authority. This research aims to bridge the gap between normative theories and empirical evidence by exploring how these participatory experiments interact with systemic power struggles. Specifically, it examines how the 2022 macro-fiscal adjustment was politicized by opposing factions to challenge the legitimacy of the proposed charter. By building and analyzing a database of these dynamics, the project will trace how institutional design choices under fiscal pressure shape the ultimate survival and public acceptance of participatory constitutions.

This argument avoids two shortcuts. The first is to treat participation as automatically democratizing. Participation can expand voice, thicken constitutional imagination, and bring excluded actors into the institutional field. Yet it can also aggregate demands without producing a governing project, especially when citizen input, party mediation, expert authority, and representative institutions remain misaligned. The second shortcut is to explain rejection as misinformation alone. Digital communication matters, but not as an external distortion added to an otherwise coherent process. It is part of the contemporary terrain on which constitutional legitimacy is built, contested, and lost. By tracing how fiscal constraints and digital platforms reshape constitutional debates, this project explicitly seeks to bridge the gap between normative theories and empirical evidence. It examines how participatory mechanisms interact with political actors, power struggles, and competing visions of democratic legitimacy, revealing the ultimate consequences of these processes for representation, accountability, and governability.

The empirical strategy is structured around the systematic collection of standardized variables that contribute directly to building and analyzing a database of constitutional designs. The project traces the movement of constitutional demands across three distinct Chilean archives: the Bachelet II participatory process (2015–2018), the 2021–2022 Constitutional Convention, and the digitally mediated public sphere. To mitigate recent Twitter/X API access restrictions and high costs, the digital archive will rely on the Meta Ad Library, open-access Gelt.vc/social archives, and partnership-based access to Spanish-language academic Twitter datasets. Rather than treating Chile as a siloed case, these archives will be coded into standard variables—measuring the design of participatory mechanisms, elite-citizen power struggles, and legitimacy narratives—that seamlessly integrate with the cross-national POPGOV database. This integration allows us to compare the Chilean case module directly with other cases, revealing patterns, drivers, and outcomes across diverse political contexts.

To make the post-doctoral scope feasible, the methodological strategy narrows the focus to a targeted mixed-methods approach. It combines comparative-historical process tracing of institutional design and legitimacy claims with computational text-as-data analysis, while replacing full-scale time-series econometric modeling with a more bounded, descriptive analysis of macro-fiscal indicators mapped against public opinion polls. This computational analysis will utilize standard topic modeling and semantic similarity measures to trace how specific constitutional demands moved from local participatory assemblies into the final text, and how they were subsequently contested. This framework is explicitly designed to bridge the gap between normative theories and empirical evidence, enabling us to systematically assess how institutional rules and external economic shocks affect representational outcomes and accountability across different stages of constitution-making.

Crucially, the political-economic layer is treated as an endogenous mechanism, not a background variable, but analyzed in a highly feasible manner. Since the 2008–2009 crisis, Chile’s growth model has faced weaker expansion and declining confidence in the transition's institutional settlement, culminating in the 2022 fiscal adjustment. Rather than attempting a complex dynamic time-series econometric model, I will construct a descriptive index of macro-fiscal constraints (growth, debt, inflation, and public expenditure) and map it chronologically against public opinion polls and Convention debates. This allows me to examine how material constraint entered the public interpretation of a proposal that promised transformation while the state appeared financially constrained, illustrating how economic realities shape competing visions of democratic legitimacy.

This research is structured to align strictly with the fellowship's 1+3 year format, ensuring a bounded and highly concrete deliverable at the end of Year 1. In the first 12 months, I will focus exclusively on coding the 2021–2022 Convention corpus and the 2022 conjuncture to build the "Chilean Case Module," yielding a clean, fully documented dataset of participatory design choices and public legitimacy contests. This module's schema will be designed in direct collaboration with the POPGOV team, ensuring that its variables (e.g., citizen submission rules, veto points, and media campaign indicators) map directly onto the broader POPGOV cross-national codebook. This approach ensures that Year 1 delivers an immediately useful contribution to building and analyzing a database, rather than a siloed case study. In Years 2 to 4, this module will be used to run comparative analyses against other Latin American and European cases in the database, allowing the project to identify patterns, drivers, and outcomes across diverse political contexts and to investigate the broader consequences of participatory design for representation and accountability.

Furthermore, my trajectory as a political economist working within traditional Economics departments has highlighted the limitations of disciplinary silos in my home country. My desire to join the POPGOV project is anchored in two complementary motivations. First, it allows me to leverage my in-depth, specialized knowledge of Chile’s historical experiments in popular government and socialist revolution, providing the project with a rigorous, data-rich case study of the Global South. Second, and equally important, it offers the ideal interdisciplinary environment to branch my profile as a social scientist beyond conventional economics. By engaging with political scientists, sociologists, and legal scholars within POPGOV, I aim to bridge the gap between normative theories and empirical evidence, contributing to broader debates on democratic innovation, institutional change, and the political economy of popular government.

A second contribution concerns popular government in the digital age, shifting the focus from abstract cybernetic models to the concrete design of modern participatory digital infrastructures. While historical precedents like Allende’s Project Cybersyn (an early attempt at democratic cybernetic planning) highlight Chile's long tradition of technological experimentation, the contemporary challenge lies in how platform capitalism and digital tools mediate constitutional deliberation. Chile’s 2021–2022 process relied on custom-built digital platforms and civic tech (such as the Convención's digital participation portal and Decidim-inspired initiatives) to crowdsource popular proposals. However, these institutional feedback loops clashed with a public sphere dominated by privately owned platforms, paid advertising, and algorithmic polarization.

This contrast is analytically productive for Track 1. Popular government today cannot depend solely on the existence of digital consultation portals; it requires understanding how the technical design of these platforms affects power struggles between elites and citizen movements. By analyzing the data flow and engagement patterns of the Chile Convención platforms alongside social media campaign dynamics, this project will examine how digital tools either democratize representation or expose participatory mechanisms to capture and legitimacy crises.

Chile’s first constitutional attempt after 2019 is often read as a failure. I propose to study it as something more useful: a case where popular government became imaginable but did not become governable. That failure belongs to a longer Chilean history in which democratic transformation has repeatedly confronted the problem of institutional mediation, from the socialist revolution under Allende and the cybernetic promise of Cybersyn to the digitally mediated constitutional politics of the present. The Chilean case therefore speaks not only to the fate of one constitutional proposal, but to a wider question: what institutional, fiscal, communicative, and digital capacities are required for popular government to become durable democratic authority?
