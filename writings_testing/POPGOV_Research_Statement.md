**Popular Government after the Guzmanian State: Participatory Constitution-Making, Fiscal Constraint, and Digital Mediation in Chile**

Chile’s first constitutional attempt after the 2019 revolt opened a rare democratic possibility. Ordinary citizens, social movements, elected representatives, experts, parties, and organized interests entered a process that promised to replace the institutional architecture inherited from dictatorship. The result was harder to interpret. A process born from mass mobilization and democratic refoundation produced an expansive constitutional proposal, but that proposal failed to secure majority legitimacy in the exit plebiscite.

This gap between participatory opening and plebiscitary rejection is the problem at the center of my proposed research. Chile shows that participation does not become popular government by institutional design alone. Citizen input must be translated into constitutional form, constitutional form into governing capacity, and governing capacity into a public sense that transformation is legitimate, coherent, and feasible. The first Chilean attempt after 2019 broke down somewhere along that chain.

The crisis did not begin with the Convention. It emerged from the long exhaustion of the post-1980 constitutional order: a state form shaped by Jaime Guzmán’s project of protected democracy, constrained sovereignty, subsidiary statehood, and anti-majoritarian insulation. I refer to this architecture as the Guzmanian state, not to reduce Chilean neoliberalism to one figure, but to name the institutional form through which neoliberal accumulation, democratic restriction, and reform without rupture were held together for four decades. By 2019, that order had not simply lost popularity. It had lost much of its capacity to process social conflict through ordinary reform.

The research asks how Chile’s 2021--2022 constitution-making process transformed a long crisis of institutional legitimacy into an expansive constitutional proposal, and why that participatory opening failed to consolidate a politically plausible project of popular government. The outcome to be explained is not participation itself. It is the failed translation from participatory mobilization into constitutional authority: the difficulty of turning citizen demands into a coherent institutional project capable of securing majority legitimacy, implementation credibility, and governing capacity.

My central hypothesis is that this failure resulted from the interaction of historical disconnection, material constraint, and communicative mediation. The Convention expanded participation and opened a field of constitutional imagination, but it remained weakly connected to earlier programmatic constitutional debates, including the process promoted during the second Bachelet administration. It gathered a wide range of social demands without fully articulating them into a shared project of self-government. At the same time, the process unfolded during a sharp macro-fiscal adjustment in 2022, which narrowed the perceived space for reform and made questions of cost, feasibility, and governability politically salient. Those tensions were not received by citizens in a neutral public sphere. They were reframed through digital platforms, paid advertising, anomalous amplification, and polarized campaign narratives that shaped how the Convention and the proposed text were perceived.

This argument avoids two shortcuts. The first is to treat participation as automatically democratizing. Participation can expand voice, thicken constitutional imagination, and bring excluded actors into the institutional field. Yet it can also aggregate demands without producing a governing project, especially when citizen input, party mediation, expert authority, and representative institutions remain misaligned. The second shortcut is to explain rejection as misinformation alone. Digital communication matters, but not as an external distortion added to an otherwise coherent process. It is part of the contemporary terrain on which constitutional legitimacy is built, contested, and lost.

The empirical strategy follows the movement of constitutional ideas across three archives. The first is the Bachelet II process: civic-education materials, local and territorial dialogues, cabildos, the \textit{Bases Ciudadanas}, the Consejo Ciudadano de Observadores, the 2017 proposal to reform Chapter XV, and the 2018 constitutional project. This archive gives the study a pre-2019 baseline, an earlier attempt to connect citizen participation, institutional reform, and constitutional replacement. The second archive is the 2021--2022 Convention: rules, committee materials, popular initiatives, debate records, draft provisions, harmonization documents, and the final proposal. The third archive reconstructs the digital public sphere through Twitter/X data and Facebook Ad Library materials, tracking how constitutional issues, fiscal-cost narratives, implementation doubts, legitimacy claims, and campaign frames changed over time.

The method combines process tracing with text-as-data analysis. Process tracing reconstructs the sequence linking the exhaustion of the Guzmanian state, the 2019 revolt, the constitutional opening, Convention deliberation, the fiscal-political conjuncture of 2022, digital campaign dynamics, and the referendum outcome. Text-as-data analysis compares the language and issue structure of the Bachelet II archive, the Convention corpus, the final proposal, and digital debate. Topic modelling, dictionary-based classification, semantic similarity measures, and time-series indicators would allow me to identify which themes travelled across arenas, which were transformed, and which broke apart between participation, drafting, campaign communication, and plebiscitary judgment.

The political-economic layer is part of the mechanism, not a background variable. Since the 2008--2009 crisis, Chile’s growth model has faced weaker expansion, distributional pressure, rising household insecurity, and declining confidence in the institutional settlement that governed the transition. The 2022 fiscal adjustment sharpened that contradiction. A constitutional process organized around social rights and state transformation unfolded while the government was also signaling fiscal discipline and limiting expenditure. I would assemble indicators of growth, distribution, labor-market conditions, fiscal expenditure, fiscal balance, debt, public investment, and inflation to study how material constraint shaped the perceived plausibility of constitutional change. The question is not whether fiscal adjustment mechanically caused rejection. It is how fiscal constraint entered the public interpretation of a proposal that promised transformation while the state appeared constrained in its capacity to deliver it.

This research would contribute to POPGOV by producing a structured Chilean case module on participatory constitution-making. The module would code participatory mechanisms, institutional design choices, actor positions, constitutional themes, legitimacy claims, digital-campaign dynamics, and outcomes. It would also support a Chile-focused article on the gap between participatory opening and plebiscitary rejection, and a comparative article on the conditions under which constitution-making becomes a viable form of popular government rather than a momentary expansion of voice.

A second contribution concerns popular government in the digital age. Here I draw on Evgeny Morozov’s notion of digital socialism, especially his argument that contemporary platform capitalism has built powerful feedback infrastructures while keeping them subordinated to private accumulation, advertising, and behavioral capture. Chile gives this question historical depth. During Allende’s government, Project Cybersyn became an early attempt to place real-time information, cybernetic coordination, and productive decision-making at the service of a democratic socialist transition. Its horizon was not merely technical planning, but the possibility that popular government could govern complexity without surrendering either to the market or to bureaucratic command. Half a century later, the first constitutional process after 2019 unfolded in a radically different digital environment. Citizen participation expanded, but the public legitimacy of the process was mediated through privately owned platforms, anomalous amplification, paid advertising, and fragmented political communication.

The contrast is analytically productive. Chile’s history links two moments that are usually studied apart: the cybernetic promise of democratic socialist planning under Allende and the platform-mediated crisis of constitutional politics in the present. Popular government today cannot depend only on consultation, deliberation, or constitutional drafting. It also requires democratic control over the infrastructures through which society detects needs, processes conflict, coordinates solutions, and listens to itself.

Chile’s first constitutional attempt after 2019 is often read as a failure. I propose to study it as something more useful: a case where popular government became imaginable but did not become governable. That failure belongs to a longer Chilean history in which democratic transformation has repeatedly confronted the problem of institutional mediation, from the socialist revolution under Allende and the cybernetic promise of Cybersyn to the digitally mediated constitutional politics of the present. The Chilean case therefore speaks not only to the fate of one constitutional proposal, but to a wider question: what institutional, fiscal, communicative, and digital capacities are required for popular government to become durable democratic authority?
